February 22 · Media & Journalism

The Black Press & Media

Freedom's Journal launched in 1827 with a mission that still resonates: 'We wish to plead our own cause. Too long have others spoken for us.' The Black press has been a lifeline, a record, an organizing tool, and a target — from Frederick Douglass's North Star to the Chicago Defender to Black social media today.

Full Story

When Samuel Cornish and John Russwurm launched Freedom's Journal on March 16, 1827, they stated their purpose in the first issue with a directness that has rarely been matched: 'We wish to plead our own cause. Too long have others spoken for us.' That sentence was written in a country where Black people could be legally owned, where the mainstream press covered Black Americans either as property or as threats, and where even abolitionist white newspapers often spoke about Black people rather than to them or with them. The Black press was founded specifically to fill that gap — to create a media infrastructure that Black Americans controlled, that reported Black life accurately, and that could be used to organize against the conditions they were fighting.

Frederick Douglass launched The North Star on December 3, 1847, naming it for the star that guided enslaved people north toward freedom. The paper, published out of Rochester, New York, ran a masthead motto that was itself a political statement: 'Right is of no Sex — Truth is of no Color — God is the Father of us all, and we are all Brethren.' Douglass used the paper to publish abolitionist arguments, document the conditions of slavery, and create a public record that the mainstream press was not providing. The North Star later merged with another paper and was renamed Frederick Douglass' Paper, which ran until 1860. After the Civil War, Douglass started the New Era, covering Reconstruction from Washington, D.C. At every stage, the Black press under Douglass was doing what it was always doing: providing coverage that the white press would not.

Ida B. Wells is one of the most important investigative journalists in American history, and she pioneered that work in the Black press. After her friend Thomas Moss and his business partners were lynched in Memphis in 1892 — killed because their grocery store was successfully competing with a white-owned store — Wells began using her newspaper, the Memphis Free Speech, to investigate and document lynchings across the South. She was the first journalist to systematically study the data and challenge the narrative that lynching was a response to sexual assault. Her investigations showed that in many documented cases, the accusation was false, or the underlying cause was economic competition, or the victim had simply violated some expectation of racial deference. While Wells was traveling, a white mob burned down her office and threatened her life. She never returned to Memphis. She continued her anti-lynching journalism from New York, then Chicago, where she lived the rest of her life.

Robert Sengstacke Abbott founded the Chicago Defender in 1905. Within a decade it was the most influential Black newspaper in the United States, with a national circulation of more than 230,000 — including copies smuggled into the South by Pullman porters, because the paper was so explicitly pro-migration and anti-Jim Crow that white Southerners tried to ban it. The Defender published employment listings for Northern factory jobs, first-person accounts of migrants who had made the move, editorials urging Black Southerners to leave, and detailed coverage of the Chicago Race Riot of 1919. Historians credit the Defender with being a significant driver of the Great Migration. It also covered the 1919 race riot, anti-Black violence in the North, and the persistent segregation that greeted migrants in their new cities. The paper's 250,000-photograph archive, now being digitized with Google Arts & Culture, is one of the most comprehensive visual records of Black urban life in 20th-century America.

John H. Johnson founded Ebony magazine in 1945 and Jet in 1951, creating the first mass-market Black publications aimed at middle-class Black consumers. Ebony reached 1.5 million readers in its first year. Jet published the photo of Emmett Till's open casket in 1955 — a decision made by Till's mother, Mamie Till-Mobley, who insisted that the world see what had been done to her son. The photo ran in Jet and was credited by civil rights leaders including Rosa Parks and John Lewis as a galvanizing moment. The mainstream white press would not have published it. Today, the tradition of the Black press has migrated substantially to social media. Black Twitter documented and amplified events including the killing of Trayvon Martin, Michael Brown, Philando Castile, Breonna Taylor, and George Floyd before or simultaneously with mainstream coverage. The hashtags #BlackLivesMatter, #SayHerName, and #BlackTwitter became organizing tools, grief communities, and information networks that function in precisely the same way the Black press always has: providing coverage, context, and community that the mainstream will not.

We wish to plead our own cause. Too long have others spoken for us.
Freedom's Journal, March 16, 1827 — first issue, first sentence
Why It Matters Today

The Black press matters because it has always done what no other institution was going to do: document Black life accurately, on Black terms, for Black audiences. This isn't a historical fact that ended when mainstream media desegregated its newsrooms. Today, Black journalists remain underrepresented in major news organizations, coverage of Black communities remains disproportionately crime-focused in mainstream outlets, and Black social media has filled in the gap the same way the Chicago Defender did in 1905. The record that the Black press has created — from Freedom's Journal through Ebony to Black Twitter — is also an irreplaceable historical archive. When Jet published the Emmett Till photo, it made it impossible to look away. That is what the Black press has always done, and why it has always been a target.
