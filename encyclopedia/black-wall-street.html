February 23 · Economics & Resilience

Black Wall Street

Before it was destroyed in 1921, Greenwood District in Tulsa, Oklahoma had 35 blocks of Black-owned businesses — banks, law offices, hotels, a hospital, a library, a school system, a bus line, and its own newspaper. It was called Black Wall Street. It was built in less than 20 years by Black Americans who had been told they were not capable of building anything.

Full Story

The economic history of Black America after emancipation is one of the most consequential and least told stories in the country. In 1865, approximately four million people were freed from slavery — people who had been legally prohibited from learning to read or write, who owned no land, who had no capital, who had no access to the legal and financial systems that would have allowed them to accumulate wealth, and who were entering a labor market in which they were immediately subject to new systems of coercive labor through sharecropping, convict leasing, and debt bondage. Within this context, the emergence of thriving Black economic communities across the South and Southwest in the late 19th and early 20th centuries is not merely impressive. It is extraordinary.

Greenwood District in Tulsa, Oklahoma was one of the most documented of these communities. O.W. Gurley, a Black educator from Arkansas, purchased land on Greenwood Avenue in 1906 and built a rental house, stipulating that it could only be sold to Black people. He then built more housing, and eventually a grocery store, and became one of the anchors around which a community formed. By the early 1920s, Greenwood had more than 600 Black-owned businesses spread across 35 blocks: hotels, law offices, banks, a hospital, a library, a school system with its own high school, a bus line, and the Tulsa Star newspaper. The community had Black physicians, Black lawyers, Black dentists, and Black insurance companies. It was described by Booker T. Washington as one of the most prosperous Black communities in the country, and by the Tulsa Tribune — with condescension — as 'Little Africa.' A Black reporter named O.B. Williams called it Black Wall Street, and the name stuck.

On May 31 and June 1, 1921, Greenwood was destroyed. The Tulsa Race Massacre — covered in the Rosewood and Tulsa massacre entry — leveled 35 blocks, burned more than 1,256 homes, and killed an unknown number of Black residents. The death toll was suppressed; estimates range from 100 to 300 or more. The massacre was triggered by an accusation against a young Black man named Dick Rowland; the charge was never substantiated, and Rowland was never convicted. The actual cause was economic: the growing wealth and independence of Greenwood threatened the racial hierarchy that white Tulsa depended on. The city's legal and political infrastructure then compounded the destruction by passing zoning laws intended to prevent Greenwood from rebuilding in the same location. The community rebuilt anyway.

The rebuilding of Greenwood after 1921 is as remarkable as what was destroyed. Within two decades, historians document a commercial district that, by some measures, was more prosperous than before the massacre. Black Tulsans pooled resources, challenged the zoning laws in court, and reconstructed their economic life through collective action and communal investment. The same pattern appears in other Black economic communities across the country: Durham, North Carolina's Hayti district; Washington, D.C.'s U Street Corridor; Atlanta's Sweet Auburn; Richmond, Virginia's Jackson Ward. Each was built through collective effort, each achieved notable prosperity under conditions of forced segregation, and each was damaged or destroyed — by urban renewal, highway construction, economic disinvestment, or violence.

The broader concept that Greenwood represents — Black economic self-sufficiency, internal investment, circulating dollars within the community — is sometimes called 'Black Wall Street' in contemporary discourse, used to describe both the historical community and an aspiration for Black economic empowerment. The economic research is consistent: a dollar spent in Black-owned businesses circulates within Black communities for a fraction of the time it would otherwise, because of structural barriers to Black business ownership and Black consumer access. The historical lesson of Greenwood is not just that a thriving Black community was built and destroyed. It is that the community rebuilt, twice, against deliberate structural resistance, and that the tools used to limit Black economic power — violence, zoning laws, redlining, discriminatory lending — have names and documented histories that connect directly to wealth gaps that persist today.

There is a lion terrorizing us, preying upon us, and upon every business effort which we put forth. The name of this insatiable lion is prejudice.
Maggie Lena Walker, Black banker and entrepreneur, 1906
Why It Matters Today

Black Wall Street matters as proof, not aspiration. It is documented historical evidence that Black Americans built thriving, self-sufficient economic communities under conditions of active oppression — and that those communities were systematically destroyed by violence and policy, not by any failure of the communities themselves. The racial wealth gap in 2024 is not an accident or an inevitability. It is the accumulated result of documented policies: the destruction of Greenwood and dozens of similar communities, redlining, discriminatory GI Bill administration, discriminatory lending, and mass incarceration. Understanding Greenwood's history means understanding that what was destroyed had to be destroyed deliberately, that it rebuilt anyway, and that the debate over reparations is partly a debate over whether the United States will ever account for that deliberate destruction.
